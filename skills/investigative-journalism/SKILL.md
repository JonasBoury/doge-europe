# Investigative Journalism Skills

## Core Principles

### The Five W's and H
Every investigation should answer:
- **Who**: All relevant actors
- **What**: What happened
- **When**: Timeline of events
- **Where**: Locations involved
- **Why**: Motivations and causes
- **How**: Methods and mechanisms

### Standards of Proof
1. **On the record**: Named source, can be quoted
2. **On background**: Information can be used but not attributed
3. **Off the record**: For context only, cannot be used
4. **Deep background**: Information can be used without any attribution

### Verification Requirements
- **Two-source rule**: Major allegations need at least two independent sources
- **Document everything**: Keep records of all research and communication
- **Assume nothing**: Verify even "obvious" facts
- **Be skeptical but fair**: Question everything but remain objective

## Source Development

### Finding Sources

**Where to Look:**
- Former employees ("latent sources")
- Competitors and rivals
- Regulatory agency contacts
- Academic experts
- Whistleblower platforms
- Industry conferences
- Professional associations

**Approach Strategies:**
- Start with peripheral sources, work inward
- Use mutual connections for introductions
- Attend public events where targets speak
- Build trust gradually
- Protect source identity from the start

### Working with Sources

**Building Trust:**
- Be transparent about your intentions
- Explain how you'll protect them
- Follow through on promises
- Respect boundaries
- Maintain regular contact

**Source Protection:**
- Use encrypted communications (Signal, ProtonMail)
- Meet in secure locations
- Remove identifying details
- Use "composite sources" when appropriate
- Never reveal sources without permission
- Be prepared for legal pressure

**Evaluating Sources:**
- What's their motive for talking?
- Do they have direct knowledge?
- Can they provide documentation?
- Do other sources corroborate?
- Are they credible and reliable?

## Interview Techniques

### Preparation
- Research subject thoroughly
- Prepare question outline (but stay flexible)
- Know the key facts already
- Anticipate deflections
- Have documents ready to reference

### Interview Strategy

**Types of Interviews:**
- **Friendly**: Building rapport with cooperative sources
- **Adversarial**: Confronting subjects with allegations
- **Ambush**: Last resort when subject won't cooperate

**Questioning Techniques:**
- Open-ended questions to start
- Specific questions for key facts
- Silence is powerful - let them fill it
- Ask "how" and "why" not just "what"
- Circle back to catch inconsistencies
- Save hardest questions for end

**Recording:**
- Always ask permission to record
- Take notes even when recording
- Record in-person when allowed
- Keep recordings securely
- Transcribe key sections

### Red Flags in Interviews
- Refusing to answer basic questions
- Overly rehearsed responses
- Deflection and topic-changing
- Attacking the investigator
- "I don't recall" to everything
- Threatening legal action prematurely

**Key Questions:**
- Is it authentic?
- Who created it and when?
- What's the context?

**Document Verification:**
- Check metadata
- Verify signatures

- Consult experts if needed
- Look for signs of manipulation
- Find corroborating documents

## Fact-Checking Process

### Verification Steps

1. **Identify the Claim**: Be specific about what you're checking
2. **Find the Source**: Track to original source when possible
3. **Seek Expert Input**: Consult relevant experts
4. **Check Multiple Sources**: Don't rely on single verification
5. **Consider Context**: Is the claim missing important context?
6. **Rate Accuracy**: Use consistent rating system

### Common Errors to Avoid
- Confirmation bias
- False balance ("both sides")
- Relying on secondary sources
- Missing important context
- Mistaking correlation for causation
- Being first instead of being right

## Safety and Security

### Digital Security

**Essential Practices:**
- Use VPN for sensitive research
- Encrypted messaging (Signal, Wire)
- Secure email (ProtonMail)
- Password manager
- Two-factor authentication
- Secure device encryption

**Operational Security:**
- Separate devices for sensitive work
- Don't discuss investigations in insecure channels
- Be aware of surveillance
- Protect source communications
- Secure physical documents
- Have backup plans

### Physical Safety
- Assess risks before pursuing story
- Tell someone where you're going
- Meet sources in safe locations
- Be aware of surveillance
- Have exit strategies
- Know when to back off

### Legal Protection
- Know your rights
- Understand relevant laws
- Have legal counsel available
- Document everything
- Keep sources confidential
- Be prepared for legal challenges

## Ethical Guidelines

### Core Ethics

**Truth Above All:**
- Accuracy is paramount
- Admit and correct errors
- Don't exaggerate or sensationalize
- Distinguish fact from opinion
- Be transparent about limitations

**Minimize Harm:**
- Balance public interest against privacy
- Protect vulnerable sources
- Consider unintended consequences
- Give subjects chance to respond
- Redact unnecessary personal information

**Independence:**
- Avoid conflicts of interest
- Disclose any relevant connections
- Don't accept gifts or favors
- Maintain editorial independence
- Resist pressure from powerful interests

**Accountability:**
- Stand behind your work
- Correct errors promptly
- Explain your methodology
- Accept criticism
- Respect legal boundaries

### Difficult Ethical Questions

**When to Publish:**
- Is it in the public interest?
- Is it verified sufficiently?
- What's the potential harm?
- Have subjects had right to respond?
- Is timing appropriate?

**Using Leaked Documents:**
- Is the leak in the public interest?
- Is the material authentic?
- Was it legally obtained by you?
- Does public interest outweigh privacy?
- Are there national security concerns?

**Deception:**
- Generally avoid deception
- Only when story of vital public interest
- When no other way to get information
- Disclose the deception in story
- Be prepared to defend the choice

## Investigation Workflow

### Phase 1: Planning (10% of time)
- Define the hypothesis
- Identify key questions
- Plan research approach
- Assess risks
- Allocate resources

### Phase 2: Research (40% of time)
- Gather documents
- Conduct interviews
- Analyze data
- Follow leads
- Build evidence

### Phase 3: Verification (30% of time)
- Cross-check all facts
- Seek additional sources
- Test alternative explanations
- Consult experts
- Verify documents

### Phase 4: Writing (15% of time)
- Organize findings clearly
- Lead with most important facts
- Provide context
- Include responses
- Show your work

### Phase 5: Legal/Ethical Review (5% of time)
- Legal review if needed
- Ethical considerations
- Fact-checking
- Right of reply
- Final verification

## Writing the Story

### Structure

**Inverted Pyramid:**
- Lead: Most important facts
- Nut graph: Why it matters
- Evidence: Supporting details
- Context: Background
- Conclusion: Implications

**Long-form Alternative:**
- Compelling opening
- Statement of findings
- Methodology note
- Detailed evidence
- Responses and reaction
- Broader significance

### Best Practices
- Show, don't just tell
- Use specific examples
- Include human impact
- Explain technical matters clearly
- Provide context
- Attribute properly
- Include caveats

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid
- Burying the lead
- Jargon and complexity
- Missing the "so what"
- Insufficient attribution
- Imbalance
- Unclear sourcing
- Overlooking context

## After Publication

### Follow-up
- Monitor reactions
- Respond to legitimate criticism
- Correct errors immediately
- Follow new leads
- Track impact
- Consider follow-up stories

### Dealing with Pushback
- Stand by accurate reporting
- Correct genuine errors
- Respond professionally to criticism
- Don't engage with bad-faith attacks
- Document threats or intimidation
- Have legal support ready
